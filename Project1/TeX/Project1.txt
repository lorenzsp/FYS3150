\begin{abstract}

We find before how to rewrite the differential equation as a linear set of equations and then we solve it using gaussian elimination and LU decomposition. \section{Methods and algortihms}


Our purpose is to discretize the one-dimensional Poisson equation (1) in order to find a numerical solution in the interval $x\in(0,1)$:
\begin{equation}
\begin{cases}
- \frac{d^2 u(x)}{dx^2} = f(x) = 100e^{-10x} \\
u(0) = 1 \\
u(1) = 0 
\end{cases}
\end{equation}
According to Taylor expansion we can rewrite the second derivative as:
\[
\frac{d^2 u(x)}{dx^2} = \frac{u(x+h) + u(x-h) - 2u(x)}{h^2} + O(h^2)
\]
we discretize the interval $(0,1)$ in $n+2$ points $x_i = i \, h$ with $h=1/(n+1)$ from $x_0 = 0$ to $x_{n+1} = 1$. If we neglect the term $O(h^2)$ the equation (1) becomes :
\begin{equation*}
   -\frac{v_{i+1}+v_{i-1}-2v_i}{h^2} = f_i  \hspace{0.5cm} \mathrm{for} \hspace{0.1cm} i=1,\dots, n,
\end{equation*}
where $f_i=f(x_i)$ and $v_i = u(x_i)$.
We  can rewrite this equation as a linear set of equations of the form
\begin{equation*}
   \mathbf{A}\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{r},
\end{equation*}
where $r_i=h^2 f_i$ and $\mathbf{A}$ is an $n\times n$  tridiagonal matrix which we rewrite as

\[
    \mathbf{A} = \begin{bmatrix}
                           2& -1& 0 &\dots   & \dots &0 \\
                           -1 & 2 & -1 &0 &\dots &\dots \\
                           0&-1 &2 & -1 & 0 & \dots \\
                           & \dots   & \dots &\dots   &\dots & \dots \\
                           0&\dots   &  &-1 &2& -1 \\
                           0&\dots    &  & 0  &-1 & 2 \\
                      \end{bmatrix},
\]
 % i think this is the request of the point 1a
\begin{subequations}
\begin{align}
 2&v_1 - 1 v_2 + 0 v_3 + 0v_4 ... = h^2 f_1 = r_1  \\
 -1&v_1 + 2 v_2 + -1 v_3 + 0v_4 ... = h^2 f_2 = r_2 \\
0&v_1 -1 v_2 + 2 v_3 + -1 v_4 +... = h^2 f_2 = r_3 \\
\quad ... \notag
\end{align}
\end{subequations}
We can use the gaussian elimination applied to this specific matrix $\mathbf{A}$. We define two new variables:
\begin{equation}
d_{i} = 2 - \frac{1}{d_{i-1}} = \frac{i+1}{i} 
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\tilde{r}_i = r_i  - \frac{\tilde{r}_{i-1}}{d_{i-1}}
\end{equation}
where $i=2,3,...,n$ and $d_1 = 2$ and $\tilde{r}_1 = r_1$. After that we obtain a new matrix with all the elements under the diagonal equal to zero.
